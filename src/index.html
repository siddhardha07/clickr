Pictures have shaped the way we see and understand the world.The invention of the camera in the 19th century revolutionized how we capture moments, leading to film photography, instant Polaroids, and eventually, the digital images we take today. With every advancement, pictures have evolved from simple drawings to high-resolution snapshots, preserving history, emotions, and creativity for generations to come.

Widely acknowledged as one of the most influential physicists of all time, Einstein is fêted (someone or something have been honored or celebrated) for developing the theory of relativity. Perhaps the best known portrait of the German-born physicist is Arthur Sasse’s light-hearted portrait.

It was Einstein’s 72nd birthday in 1951 and he celebrated at The Princeton Club. Goaded to smile for the camera, he impetuously stuck out his tongue, with Sasse on hand to snap the iconic shot, while other photographers surrounding the car missed it.

Showcasing a light and nutty professor side to the celebrated scientist rather than the serious one that many assumed about the man, the photo softened the perception of Einstein, which he seemingly liked as he ordered nine copies. A signed copy sold at auction sold for $72,300, making it the most expensive Einstein photograph ever sold.

Microsoft has an insect infestation to thank for its classic Windows XP desktop background—an image that has graced at least a billion computer screens since the system’s 2001 launch.

It was this vision of Sonoma County that flashed by Charles O’Rear’s car window as he drove down Highway 121 in 1998. A few years later, he got a call from Microsoft asking to use his shot of Sonoma County O’Rear agreed to sell Microsoft all the rights to his photograph.

Microsoft never officially disclosed the amount they paid for the photo. However, O'Rear hinted that the deal was one of the largest amounts ever paid for a single photograph. Some estimates suggest it was in the six-figure range ($100,000 or more). The payment was so large that no shipping company would insure the original film, so Microsoft flew him to their headquarters to deliver it in person.
